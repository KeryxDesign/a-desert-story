How it all begins

Now, let me tell you about a nine-year-old boy named Sebastiano, who lived in the village of Montefiore, tucked among cheerful green hills, at the edge of a wise and ancient forest. His mother was a skilled herbalist, and from her Sebastiano had learned the names of the plants, their moods and their remedies. But there was something few people knew about him: a rather astonishing secret.

Sebastiano could speak with the animals. Every last one of them, believe me.

The gift had come from a Sorceress, and it came with a promise to keep: those powers were meant to protect the forests, never to serve his own whims. And Sebastiano had promised, though sometimes, I must say, the urge to tell his friends everything was almost too much to bear!

Then one day came a piece of news no one wanted to believe. Far away, beyond the dunes, beyond where the desert seems never to end, there stood a palace no one could see. And inside that palace, a Magician kept the animals prisoner, one after another.

So Sebastiano set off. With Baltazar, a camel who, you should know, had an opinion about very nearly everything, and with a few companions gathered along the way, the kind you don't expect and then never leave behind.

Inside the palace, the strangest trial of all was waiting for him: seven doors, one after another. Not one of them opened by force. Each door guarded a riddle, set there by an ancient Sage, and it opened only with the right answer. And the questions, before those doors, were the kind that make you scratch your head and stop to think, truly think.

The rest, the book will tell you. I have only opened the first door for you.

The characters

Who you'll meet in the desert

Every one of them was drawn by hand, by Sybil.

Sebastiano

Nine years old, with a secret gift: he speaks with every animal in the world. He sets off across the desert to free the prisoners.

Baltazar

The camel who calls himself King of the Camels of the East. Greedy, a little clumsy, and with an opinion about very nearly everything.

Freeze

A desert sprite with a frosty stare. Funny, touchy, exquisitely fussy at the table, and able to work a spell or two.

Fenix

A desert fox who can take human form. Guide of the land: watchful gaze, few words, and plenty of intuition.

Camaleon

Not your ordinary chameleon: a Chame-Lion. He vanishes by blending in, turns hypnotic, and has a soft spot for turquoise.

Simurg

Queen of the desert eagles. Vast, white, regal. She flies high and sees far, where no one else can reach.

Nina

A siren-frog, rare and held to be sacred, with a voice that enchants. She dreams of an audience, and perhaps of more.

Flora

A girl with copper hair and a clear, bright gaze. Pale, shut away in a room of the palace. Who is she, really?

Who will tell you this story

Sybil Stonecutter

This book has a voice, and the voice has a name: Sybil. She is the one who takes you by the hand with "now, let me tell you", like the storytellers of the Thousand and One Nights.

She tells stories gathered on her travels, in faraway places, and stitches them together for anyone willing to listen. And she is also the one who drew, by hand, the eight plates in the book: the characters one by one.

Of Sebastiano she says only this: that it is the story she holds dearest.

The right questions

Before you say yes

What formats does it come in?
Paperback with an illustrated cover and colour interiors, plus the Kindle edition.
How long is it?
Just under 300 pages, but laid out with room to breathe: airy pages, lines that don't crowd the eye, the kind a child turns happily. A whole novel, with a beginning, seven stages and an ending it has earned. Want to see how it reads? Download the extract
What age is it for?
From 8 to 12 on their own, from 7 up when read aloud. The sweet spot is between 8 and 11.
Isn't it too hard?
No. The thinking happens inside the adventure: the child solves the trials right alongside Sebastiano, without ever noticing they're learning to reason.
Who wrote the book?
Sybil, a storyteller in the spirit of the Thousand and One Nights. It's a deliberate choice: the narrating voice has a name and a presence. She is also the illustrator of the plates.
